This  provides a clear introduction to two major compliance frameworks that IT professionals must understand: **GDPR** (data privacy and localization) and **PCI DSS** (payment card data security). It explains why compliance matters, the potential consequences of non‑compliance, and the key requirements of each framework.



---

### Compliance Frameworks Cheat Sheet

This guide covers the purpose of compliance, data localization, and the two most common regulatory/industry standards: GDPR and PCI DSS.

---

#### Why Compliance Matters

| Aspect | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Sources of Compliance** | Laws (national, state, local), industry standards, organizational policies, contractual obligations. |
| **Consequences of Non‑Compliance** | Fines, incarceration, loss of employment, loss of business licenses (e.g., inability to process credit cards), reputational damage. |
| **Scope** | Can be domestic (within a country) or international (e.g., EU regulations affecting any company doing business with EU citizens). |

---

#### Data Localization

**Data localization** is the requirement that data collected within a specific country or region must remain stored within that same geographic boundary.

| Aspect | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Purpose** | Protects citizen data from foreign surveillance, ensures local legal jurisdiction over the data. |
| **Example** | Data on EU citizens must stay within the EU (GDPR). |
| **Challenge** | Global companies must build or rent data centers in each region where they collect data. |

---

#### GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

The GDPR is an EU regulation that governs the protection and privacy of personal data of EU residents. It applies to **any organization worldwide** that collects or processes data on EU citizens.

| Aspect | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Jurisdiction** | European Union (but applies globally to any company handling EU citizen data). |
| **Protected Data** | Name, address, photo, email, bank information, IP addresses, browsing history, and any other personally identifiable information (PII). |
| **Key Requirements** | Data must be stored within the EU (data localization); users have control over their data; users can request deletion ("right to be forgotten"). |
| **Core Principle** | Individuals own their own data and have the right to control how it is collected, stored, and used. |

**"Right to be Forgotten":**
- EU citizens can request that an organization delete all of their personal data.
- The organization must comply unless there is a compelling legal reason to retain it (e.g., for fraud investigation).

**Practical Impact:**
- Companies must be able to locate, export, and delete a specific user's data upon request.
- Data cannot be moved outside the EU without explicit consent or legal safeguards.

#### PCI DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard)

PCI DSS is an **industry standard** (not a law) created by the Payment Card Industry to protect credit cardholder data. It applies to any organization that stores, processes, or transmits credit card information.

| Aspect | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Governing Body** | Payment Card Industry Security Standards Council (PCI SSC). |
| **Legal Status** | Not a law, but non‑compliance can result in fines, increased transaction fees, or loss of the ability to process credit card payments (which can put a business out of operation). |
| **Audits** | Organizations are audited to verify compliance. |

**The Six PCI DSS Goals (with example controls):**

| Goal | Description | Example Controls |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. Build and Maintain Secure Networks and Systems** | Protect data in transit and at rest. | Firewalls, secure configurations, change control. |
| **2. Protect Cardholder Data** | Encrypt stored data and protect it from unauthorized access. | Encryption at rest, tokenization, truncation. |
| **3. Maintain a Vulnerability Management Program** | Identify and patch security weaknesses. | Regular vulnerability scans, timely patch management. |
| **4. Implement Strong Access Control Measures** | Restrict access to cardholder data to only those who need it. | Least privilege, multi‑factor authentication, unique IDs per user. |
| **5. Regularly Monitor and Test Networks** | Ensure security controls are working as expected. | Continuous monitoring, penetration testing, log analysis. |
| **6. Maintain an Information Security Policy** | Document and enforce security policies across the organization. | Written security policy, risk assessments, annual reviews. |

---

#### GDPR vs. PCI DSS Comparison

| Aspect | GDPR | PCI DSS |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Type** | Law (EU regulation) | Industry standard |
| **Scope** | All personal data of EU citizens | Credit cardholder data only |
| **Applicability** | Any organization handling EU citizen data | Any organization storing/processing credit cards |
| **Data Localization** | Yes – data must stay within EU | No specific localization requirement |
| **Right to Delete** | Yes ("right to be forgotten") | No – retention may be required for fraud investigation |
| **Enforcement** | EU data protection authorities; fines up to €20M or 4% of global revenue | Card brands (Visa, Mastercard, etc.); fines or loss of processing ability |

---

#### Key Takeaways

| Takeaway | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Compliance is mandatory** | Non‑compliance can lead to fines, legal action, or loss of business capability. |
| **Data localization restricts data movement** | Some laws require that data stays within the country/region where it was collected. |
| **GDPR protects EU citizen data** | Applies globally; gives individuals control over their personal data, including the right to be forgotten. |
| **PCI DSS protects credit card data** | Industry standard with six core goals; non‑compliance can result in losing the ability to process credit cards. |
| **Know which frameworks apply to your organization** | Depending on your industry and customer base, you may need to comply with one, several, or many different regulations/standards. |
