Software as a Service (SaaS)
Software as a Service is commonly associated with software that is on demand. You open a login screen in your browser, log in, and now you have access to the application. Someone else has written the application, someone else manages the application, and your data is stored somewhere within that application. You do not have a local installation of software, you do not manage or upgrade any of the software itself, and you simply log in and use the app.

This allows for centralized management of the application and the data you are putting into it, meaning everything is stored on a third-party cloud platform. This is a complete application offering, and you do not do any development work to use the app.

A good example of SaaS is Google Mail or Office 365, where you simply log in and use the app. You have no control over the underlying infrastructure, updates, or maintenance.

Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS)
There may be times when you want to build your own application, deploy it, and manage your own data, but you only need the computing resources to run that application. In this case, you want to use Infrastructure as a Service, sometimes referred to as Hardware as a Service because you are effectively using hardware in the cloud and then using your own software on top of that.

With IaaS, you are responsible for installing your software and managing all the data associated with that software. If the software needs to be upgraded, you are the one to upgrade it. The application you are using and the data you are putting into that application is still in the cloud, but you have much more control over the access and security of that data.

A good example of IaaS is web service providers where you can buy time on a particular web server, and they make that server available to everyone on the internet. You control what runs on the server and manage the operating system and applications.

Platform as a Service (PaaS)
A middle ground between SaaS and IaaS is Platform as a Service. This is still in the cloud, but you are the one who manages all the development efforts to customize the application to your specifications.

The cloud provider gives you all the tools necessary to build this particular app. You can think of this as a series of building blocks provided by the platform as a service infrastructure, and then you build out those blocks to create your own application.

The underlying engine that runs the application is the responsibility of the platform as a service provider. Your responsibility is to build and maintain the apps running on the platform.

A good example of PaaS is something like Salesforce, where they give you all the tools necessary to create a customized application running on their platform. You focus on application logic and data, while the provider handles the underlying infrastructure.

Cloud Responsibility Matrix
The level of responsibility you have depends on which cloud service model you choose.

On-Premises (Traditional) Everything from the data to the applications to the physical data center is the responsibility of the customer. You own and manage everything.

Infrastructure as a Service The cloud provider supplies the physical data center, physical network, and physical host. You handle the operating system, applications, data, and all management above that layer.

Platform as a Service Network controls, applications, and identity infrastructure may be shared between the customer and the cloud provider. You are still responsible for accounts, devices, and your data.

Software as a Service The provider handles almost everything, including the application itself. You are responsible for your accounts, your data, and how you access the service.

Choosing the Right Model
The cloud model you choose depends on how much control you want versus how much management you want to avoid. SaaS gives you the least responsibility but also the least control. IaaS gives you the most control but requires the most management. PaaS provides a balance where you focus on building your application while the provider handles the underlying platform.

Consider what your team has the expertise to manage, what level of customization you need, and what your budget allows when selecting the right cloud service model for your application deployment.
