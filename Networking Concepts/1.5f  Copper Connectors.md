This  provides a clear and practical guide to the most common copper network connectors you'll encounter. It covers their physical appearance, pin configurations, and typical use cases.



### Copper Connector Types Cheat Sheet

This guide covers the four main copper connectors used in networking: RJ11, RJ45, F-type, and BNC.

---

#### Common Connector Types

| Connector | Full Name / Standard | Description / Key Features | Pins / Conductors | Common Use Cases |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **RJ11** | **Registered Jack Type 11** | A small, narrow connector. It is a 6-position connector but typically uses only the 2 center conductors. | **6P2C** (6 Position, 2 Conductor) | Analog telephones, DSL (Digital Subscriber Line) connections. |
| **RJ45** | **Registered Jack Type 45** | A wider, 8-position connector that is slightly larger than RJ11. It is the standard connector for Ethernet networking. | **8P8C** (8 Position, 8 Conductor) | **Ethernet** (wired networking) for computers, switches, routers, and other network devices. |
| **F-Type** | **F Connector** | A threaded, cylindrical connector used for coaxial cable. It screws on for a very secure, weather-resistant connection. | Single center pin (coax) | **Cable modems**, television/cable TV connections, DOCSIS (Data Over Cable Service Interface Specification) infrastructure. |
| **BNC** | **Bayonet Neill-Concelman** | A round connector with a bayonet-style locking mechanism. You push it on and twist to lock it in place. | Single center pin (coax) | Older networking (like 10BASE2), video connections, some WAN (Wide Area Network) connections, test equipment. |

---

#### Visual Comparison & Key Notes

- **RJ11 vs. RJ45:** These look very similar but are different sizes.
    - **RJ11** is smaller and plugs into telephone and DSL ports.
    - **RJ45** is larger and plugs into Ethernet ports.
- **F-Type (DOCSIS):** This is the standard screw-on connector you find on the back of a cable modem or the wall plate for cable TV/internet. The name **DOCSIS** refers to the standard for data transmission over cable, but the physical connector is the F-type.
- **BNC Locking:** The bayonet lock of the BNC connector is a key feature. A simple quarter-turn secures it, making it resistant to vibration and accidental disconnection, which is why it's still used in some specialized equipment.
