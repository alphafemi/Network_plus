 Network Cabling Types and Standards

Cabling is the foundation of everything we do in networking. Whether using routers, switches, or wireless access points, cabling is critical for communication.

---

## Importance of Cabling

*   **Foundation:** Cabling is a critical part of infrastructure communication.
*   **Permanence:** Once installed, cabling is difficult to remove or replace.
*   **Throughput:** Even wireless networks eventually need to connect to cables. Proper cabling ensures the best possible network throughput.

---

## Twisted Pair Copper Cables

This is the most common cable type for wired Ethernet connections.

*   **Construction:** Consists of multiple wires twisted together within a single sheath.
*   **Pairs:** Cables are often paired together (transmit plus/minus, receive plus/minus).
*   **Interference Protection:** The twist ensures at least one wire is always moving away from interference. Signals are compared on the receiving end to correct errors.
*   **Twist Rates:** Different cable pairs have different twist rates.
*   **Speed Myth:** The cable itself does not have a speed. The copper is just a medium. The specific Ethernet standard determines the data throughput.

---

## Cable Categories

To determine the appropriate cable for a specific Ethernet standard, we use categories.

*   **Common Categories:** Category 5 (Cat5), Category 6 (Cat6), Category 7 (Cat7).
*   **Standards:** The IEEE 802.3 standards specify the minimum cable category required.
*   **Example:** 1000BASE-T requires a minimum of Category 5 cable. Installing Cat5 or higher will work properly.
*   **Selection:** Always check the Ethernet standard to ensure the cable type supports the expected signal and throughput.

---

## Coaxial and Twinax Cables

While twisted pair is common for local area networks, other cable types are used for specific purposes.

### Coaxial Cable
*   **Structure:** Two or more forms share a common axis (conductor, insulator, shielding, jacket).
*   **Common Use:** RG-6 cable is common for cable modems and internet connections.

### Twinax (Twin Axial)
*   **Structure:** Two conductors inside the cable.
*   **Use:** Commonly associated with 10 Gigabit Ethernet (SFP+ interface).
*   **Performance:** Full duplex communication, low cost, low latency.
*   **Distance:** Limited to about 5 meters.

## Plenum Spaces and Safety

Cabling safety is critical, especially in shared air spaces.

### What is a Plenum?
*   **Definition:** The space between a drop ceiling and the actual ceiling.
*   **Usage:** Often used for network cables, power cables, and HVAC ductwork.
*   **Air Flow:** In some environments, this space is used for air supply and return for heating or cooling systems.

### Plenum-Rated Cable
*   **Requirement:** Required when installing cables in a shared plenum space.
*   **Material:** Manufactured with Fluorinated Ethylene Polymer (FEP) or low-smoke Polyvinyl Chloride (PVC).
*   **Safety:** Designed to be much safer in a fire. Produces less smoke and hazardous fumes compared to standard PVC cable.
*   **Flexibility:** Plenum-rated cable may be less flexible than non-plenum-rated cable, making installation around twists and turns more challenging.

### Installation Considerations
*   **Fire Hazard:** Non-plenum cable in a plenum space can distribute smoke and fumes throughout the building during a fire.
*   **Verification:** Always check where the cable is being run (new or existing building) and use the correct type of cable for that location.

---

## Summary

*   **Twisted Pair:** Most common for Ethernet. Uses twisting to reduce interference.
*   **Categories:** Cat5, Cat6, Cat7. Standards dictate minimum requirements.
*   **Coaxial/Twinax:** Used for specific connections like cable modems or 10GbE.
*   **Plenum:** Shared air space requiring fire-safe (FEP/low-smoke PVC) cabling.
*   **Safety:** Always use the correct cable type for the location to ensure safety and performance.
