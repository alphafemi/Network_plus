 Fiber Optic Connector Types Documentation

Fiber optic networks utilize various connector types to ensure secure and efficient connections. This guide covers common fiber connectors: SC, LC, ST, and MPO/MTP. Based on the transcript, selecting the correct connector is critical, and each type features specific locking mechanisms to prevent accidental disconnection.

## Overview

Fiber optic connectors must match the interface on the device to ensure proper connectivity. Different connectors offer varying sizes, locking mechanisms, and fiber capacities.

- **Importance**: Using the wrong connector type can prevent connection or cause signal loss.
- **Locking Mechanisms**: Designed to prevent accidental dislodging, especially in racks with many cables.
- **Configuration**: Connectors often support individual fibers or pairs (transmit/receive).

## SC Connector (Subscriber Connector)

The SC connector is a common fiber connector type used in data centers.

- **Names**: Also known as Square Connector or Standard Connector.
- **Shape**: Square when viewed.
- **Locking Mechanism**: Pushes into the connection and snaps in place. To remove, pull on the connector to unlock.
- **Usage**: Common in data centers; supports individual fibers or pairs (one transmit, one receive).
- **Security**: Does not accidentally slip out once locked.

## LC Connector (Local Connector)

The LC connector is a smaller alternative to the SC connector.

- **Names**: Also known as Lucent Connector or Little Connector.
- **Size**: Slightly smaller than SC.
- **Locking Mechanism**: Has a clip on the top. Push down on the clip to release the lock and remove.
- **Usage**: Supports individual fibers or pairs (simultaneous plug-in for transmit/receive).
- **Security**: Prevents accidental removal; clip must be depressed to unlock.

## ST Connector (Straight Tip)

The ST connector uses a bayonet-style locking mechanism.

- **Names**: Straight Tip.
- **Locking Mechanism**: Push into the interface and give a slight twist to lock. Reverse the twist to remove.
- **Structure**: Features a protective ferrule around the fiber.
- **Usage**: Common in environments with many cables; locking mechanism prevents accidental dislodging.
- **Security**: Twist lock ensures it stays in place unless intentionally reversed.

## MPO/MTP Connector (Multi-fiber Push On)

The MPO connector is designed for high-density connections, allowing multiple fibers in a single connection.

- **Names**: MPO (Multi-fiber Push On); MTP (name provided by Corning, often referred to as MTP MPO).
- **Capacity**: Contains 12 individual fibers in a single cable connection.
- **Locking Mechanism**: Similar to SC; push in place, pull slightly to unlock.
- **Efficiency**: More efficient than single-fiber connectors; puts more fiber into a smaller connection.
- **View**: Side view shows lock on top; straight-on view shows 12 fibers.

## Comparison of Fiber Connectors

| Connector | Full Name | Size | Locking Mechanism | Fiber Count | Common Use |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **SC** | Subscriber Connector | Square | Push-Pull (Snap) | 1 or 2 (Pair) | Data Centers |
| **LC** | Local Connector | Small | Clip (Push Down) | 1 or 2 (Pair) | High Density |
| **ST** | Straight Tip | Medium | Bayonet (Twist) | 1 | Legacy/Racks |
| **MPO** | Multi-fiber Push On | Compact | Push-Pull (Snap) | 12 | High Density/Parallel |

## Summary

Fiber optic connectors vary in size, locking mechanisms, and fiber capacity. SC and LC connectors are common for single or dual fiber connections, with LC being smaller. ST connectors use a twist-lock mechanism, while MPO/MTP connectors allow for 12 fibers in a single connection for higher density. Always ensure the connector type matches the interface to maintain network integrity.
