
    # Wireless Networking Standards and Technologies

    Wireless networks are a fundamental part of modern connectivity. Whether at work or home, understanding the standards and technologies behind them helps in troubleshooting and planning.

    ---

    ## Wi-Fi Standards

    Wireless networks are governed by international standards to ensure compatibility and performance.

    *   **IEEE 802.11:** The Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) manages the 802 series standards committee. Specifically, 802.11 refers to the standards associated with wireless networks.
    *   **Wi-Fi Alliance:** This organization tests devices to ensure interoperability with IEEE standards. Devices that pass testing display the Wi-Fi trademark logo.
    *   **Naming Convention:**
        *   Historically, networks were referred to by their standard name (e.g., 802.11b, 802.11ax).
        *   Newer naming conventions use generational names (e.g., Wi-Fi 6) to make them easier to understand.
        *   **Wi-Fi 1 to Wi-Fi 3:** Corresponds to 802.11a, b, and g (rarely used today).
        *   **Wi-Fi 4 to Wi-Fi 7:** Corresponds to 802.11n through 802.11ax/be.
    *   **Frequencies:** Standards operate on 2.4GHz, 5GHz, and 6GHz ranges.
    *   **Performance:** Newer standards generally offer increased link rates due to improvements like more antennas and radios.

    ---

    ## Mobile Networks

    Mobile devices use wireless provider networks that differ from local Wi-Fi standards.

    *   **4G and LTE:**
        *   **LTE (Long Term Evolution):** A conversion between GSM and CDMA standards into a single standard for providers.
        *   **Speed:** Supports download rates of approximately 150 Mbps.
        *   **LTE Advanced (LTE-A):** An improved version that doubles download rates to 300 Mbps.
    *   **5G:**
        *   **Introduction:** Introduced in 2020 to greatly improve mobile network performance.
        *   **Speed:** Goal is 10 Gbps throughput, though realistic speeds range from 100 to 900 Mbps.
        *   **Benefits:**
            *   Similar bandwidth to wired home networks.
            *   Increased Internet of Things (IoT) footprint due to higher bandwidth.
            *   Faster data transfer and notifications.
            *   Enhanced cloud processing capabilities.

    ---

    ## Satellite Networking

    Satellite networking provides connectivity for remote locations without traditional internet access.

    *   **Function:** Uses a satellite dish to send and receive data from satellites in orbit.
    *   **Speeds:**
        *   Download: Approximately 100 Mbps.
        *   Upload: Approximately 5 Mbps.
    *   **Latency:**
        *   **Traditional:** Approximately 500 milliseconds (250ms up and 250ms down).
        *   **Newer Technologies (e.g., Starlink):** Advertises 40 milliseconds, working toward 20 milliseconds.
    *   **Challenges:**
        *   **Cost and Complexity:** Adds additional expense and setup complexity.
        *   **Line of Sight:** Requires a direct line of sight to the satellite. Obstructions can decrease performance or cause loss of connectivity.
        *   **Weather:** Storms can cause "rain fade," where connectivity is lost during the storm.

    ---

    ## Summary

    *   **Wi-Fi:** Local wireless standards managed by IEEE and tested by the Wi-Fi Alliance.
    *   **Mobile:** Wide-area wireless networks (4G, 5G) providing high-speed internet on the go.
    *   **Satellite:** Non-terrestrial connectivity for remote areas, subject to latency and weather constraints.
    *   **Evolution:** All wireless technologies are constantly evolving to increase speed and reliability.
