This  provides a crucial overview of the physical and data link layer technologies that enable wireless communication, both in local (Wi-Fi) and wide area (cellular, satellite) contexts. It explains the standards, generations, and practical considerations for each.



### Wireless Networking Standards Cheat Sheet

This guide covers the major wireless networking technologies, from your local Wi-Fi to global satellite connectivity.

---

#### Wi-Fi (IEEE 802.11 Standards)

The Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) defines the 802.11 standards for Wireless Local Area Networks (WLANs). The **Wi-Fi Alliance** tests and certifies devices for interoperability, using the trademarked "Wi-Fi" logo.

**Wi-Fi Generations & Standards:**

| 802.11 Standard | Generational Name | Frequencies Used | Max Theoretical Link Rate | Status / Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **802.11a** | (Wi-Fi 1 - unofficial) | 5 GHz | 54 Mbps | Legacy / Rarely used today. |
| **802.11b** | (Wi-Fi 2 - unofficial) | 2.4 GHz | 11 Mbps | Legacy / Rarely used today. |
| **802.11g** | (Wi-Fi 3 - unofficial) | 2.4 GHz | 54 Mbps | Legacy / Rarely used today. |
| **802.11n** | **Wi-Fi 4** | 2.4 GHz & 5 GHz | Up to 600 Mbps | Introduced MIMO (Multiple-Input Multiple-Output) for better performance. |
| **802.11ac** | **Wi-Fi 5** | 5 GHz | Up to 6.9 Gbps | Significant speed increase, wider channels. |
| **802.11ax** | **Wi-Fi 6 / Wi-Fi 6E** | 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, & 6 GHz (for 6E) | Up to 9.6 Gbps | Improved efficiency in dense environments, better power management. |
| **802.11be** | **Wi-Fi 7** | 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, & 6 GHz | Up to 46 Gbps | The latest standard (as of 2024), focused on ultra-high speeds and low latency. |

**Key Takeaway:** Newer Wi-Fi generations (Wi-Fi 4 and above) offer significantly higher speeds, operate on multiple frequency bands, and use advanced technologies like MIMO to improve performance and network capacity.

---

#### Cellular (Mobile) Networking

These are Wide Area Network (WAN) technologies provided by mobile carriers for smartphones, tablets, and IoT devices.

| Technology | Generation | Description | Typical Performance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **LTE** | **4G** | **Long Term Evolution.** A major standard that unified previous technologies (GSM, CDMA) into a single, global standard. | ~150 Mbps |
| **LTE-Advanced (LTE-A)** | **4G+** | An enhanced version of LTE, offering significantly faster speeds and better efficiency. | ~300 Mbps |
| **5G** | **5G** | The latest generation, introduced around 2020. Designed for ultra-high speeds, low latency, and massive device connectivity (IoT). | **Goal:** Up to 10 Gbps. <br> **Real-world:** 100 - 900 Mbps (and increasing). |

**Key Takeaway:** 5G is a transformative technology that aims to bring fiber-like speeds and low latency to mobile networks, enabling advanced applications like autonomous vehicles, remote surgery, and massive IoT deployments.

---

#### Satellite Networking (Non-Terrestrial)

Used for remote connectivity where terrestrial options (cable, fiber, cellular) are unavailable.

| Feature | Traditional Satellite (e.g., Geostationary) | Modern LEO Satellite (e.g., Starlink) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Orbit** | Geostationary (high orbit, ~35,000 km). | Low Earth Orbit (LEO, ~500 - 2,000 km). |
| **Latency** | **High (~500-600 ms).** Due to the long distance to space and back. | **Low (20-50 ms).** Much shorter distance. |
| **Speed** | Reasonable (e.g., 100 Mbps down / 5 Mbps up). | High (comparable to broadband). |
| **Reliability** | Susceptible to **"rain fade"** (signal degradation during heavy rain/snow). | Also susceptible to weather, but constellations of satellites offer better path diversity. |
| **Line of Sight** | **Critical.** A clear view of the sky is required. Obstructions (trees, buildings) will block the signal. | **Critical.** A clear view of the sky is required for the dish to connect to moving satellites. |

**Key Takeaway:** Satellite networking is a vital backup or primary connection for remote areas, but it comes with unique challenges of latency (especially older geostationary satellites) and weather susceptibility (rain fade). Newer Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellite systems are dramatically improving latency and speed.
