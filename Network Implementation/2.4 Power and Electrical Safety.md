### Power and Electrical Safety Cheat Sheet

This guide covers the electrical fundamentals and power protection devices essential for managing network infrastructure.

---

#### Electrical Safety First

Electricity poses a serious risk. Always prioritize safety when working with network equipment.

| Rule | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Disconnect before working** | Always unplug devices before opening them or working with internal components. |
| **Beware of stored charge** | Capacitors in devices like power supplies, monitors, and laser printers can hold a lethal charge even when unplugged. |
| **Never become the ground path** | Avoid intentionally or unintentionally connecting your body to an electrical ground while working with live circuits. |
| **Respect electricity** | Following basic safety practices keeps you and others safe. |

---

#### Electrical Fundamentals

Understanding basic electrical terms is essential for reading device specifications and planning power infrastructure.

| Term | Abbreviation | Definition | Water Analogy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Amperage (Amps)** | A | The **rate** of electron flow past a point. The amount of current. | **Diameter of the hose** – larger diameter allows more water flow. |
| **Voltage (Volts)** | V | The **pressure** or force pushing electrons through a circuit. | **Water pressure** – higher pressure pushes water harder. |
| **Wattage (Watts)** | W | The measure of **power** consumed or produced. | Calculated as: **Volts × Amps = Watts** |

**Example Calculation:**
- Device connected to 120V supply using 0.5A of current.
- Power consumption = 120V × 0.5A = **60 watts**.

---

#### Alternating Current (AC) vs. Direct Current (DC)

| Type | Full Name | Characteristics | Symbol | Common Use |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **AC** | Alternating Current | Current changes direction periodically (sine wave). Easy to transmit over long distances. | ∼ (wavy line) | Wall outlets, building power distribution. |
| **DC** | Direct Current | Current flows in a single, constant direction. | ⎓ (solid line over dashed line) | Electronics, computer components, networking devices. |

**Regional AC Standards:**

| Region | Voltage | Frequency |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| US, Canada, parts of Americas | 110–120 VAC | 60 Hz (cycles per second) |
| Europe, most of Asia, Africa | 220–240 VAC | 50 Hz |

**Conversion:** Most network devices have internal power supplies that convert incoming AC to the DC voltages required by their components (e.g., 12V, 5V, 3.3V).

#### Power Protection Devices

| Device | Full Name | Primary Function | How It Works | Best For |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **UPS** | Uninterruptible Power Supply | Provides battery backup during power loss; also conditions power (surge protection, voltage regulation). | Contains batteries that provide power when the main supply fails. | Critical network equipment (switches, routers, servers). |
| **PDU** | Power Distribution Unit | Distributes power to multiple devices; often provides remote management and individual outlet control. | Rack-mounted unit with multiple outlets; can be monitored and controlled over the network. | Data centers, server rooms, remote sites requiring power cycling of equipment. |

---

#### UPS Types (Uninterruptible Power Supply)

| Type | Also Known As | Operation | Pros | Cons |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Offline/Standby** | Standby UPS | Sits idle until power fails, then switches to battery (millisecond delay). | Least expensive, efficient. | Switching gap may cause device reboot. |
| **Line-Interactive** | — | Continuously regulates voltage; boosts or lowers voltage during brownouts/surges without switching to battery. | Good for areas with frequent brownouts. | Still switches to battery during full outages (small delay). |
| **Online/Double-Conversion** | True Online UPS | Devices always run from battery; input power continuously recharges batteries. | **Zero transfer time** – no switching gap. Provides pure, conditioned power. | Most expensive, less efficient (runs constantly). |

**Key UPS Features:**
- **Battery capacity** – determines runtime (minutes to hours).
- **Automatic shutdown** – can signal connected servers to shut down gracefully when batteries are low.
- **Number of outlets** – varies by model.
- **Surge protection** – often includes jacks for phone lines, network cables, etc.

---

#### Power Distribution Unit (PDU)

A PDU is more than a power strip. Enterprise PDUs offer intelligent, remote management.

| Feature | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Rack-Mountable** | Designed to fit in standard 19" racks (often 1U or 0U vertical). |
| **Remote Management** | Connects to network via Ethernet, has an IP address, and runs a web server for configuration. |
| **Per-Outlet Control** | Can remotely power off or power on individual outlets – useful for rebooting hung equipment. |
| **Monitoring** | Tracks power consumption, current draw, and environmental conditions. |

**Use Case:** Rebooting a remote router. Instead of sending someone to the data center, you log into the PDU and cycle power to that specific outlet.

---

#### Key Takeaways

| Takeaway | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Safety first** | Always disconnect power before working on equipment. Beware of stored charges in capacitors. |
| **Understand the basics** | Volts × Amps = Watts. AC from walls; DC powers components. |
| **UPS provides backup power** | Three main types: Offline (cheapest, switching gap), Line-Interactive (voltage regulation), Online (zero gap, most expensive). |
| **PDU enables remote power management** | Essential for data centers and remote sites; allows individual outlet control over the network. |
| **Regional power standards vary** | US uses 110-120V/60Hz; Europe uses 220-240V/50Hz. Always verify before installing equipment. |
