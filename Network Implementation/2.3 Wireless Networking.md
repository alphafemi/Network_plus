### Wireless Networking (802.11) Cheat Sheet

This guide covers the core concepts of Wi-Fi networking, including standards, frequencies, channels, bandwidth, and features that enable efficient operation.

---

#### Wireless Standards and Naming

The IEEE 802.11 committee defines wireless networking standards. The Wi-Fi Alliance introduced simpler generational names to replace technical 802.11 designations.

| 802.11 Standard | Wi-Fi Generation | Frequency Bands |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 802.11ac | **Wi-Fi 5** | 5 GHz |
| 802.11ax | **Wi-Fi 6 / Wi-Fi 6E** | 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, 6 GHz (6E) |
| 802.11be | **Wi-Fi 7** | 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, 6 GHz |

Future releases will continue the naming sequence (Wi-Fi 8, Wi-Fi 9, etc.).

---

#### Frequencies, Channels, and Bandwidth

Wireless networks operate in specific frequency ranges, divided into **channels** to organize usage.

| Frequency Band | Characteristics | Channel Widths |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **2.4 GHz** | Widely supported, more crowded, limited non-overlapping channels (typically 3). | 20 MHz (primary), 40 MHz |
| **5 GHz** | More channels, less interference, shorter range than 2.4 GHz. | 20 MHz, 40 MHz, 80 MHz, 160 MHz |
| **6 GHz** | Newest band (Wi-Fi 6E/7), very wide channels, minimal interference. | 20 MHz, 40 MHz, 80 MHz, 160 MHz |

**Channels vs. Frequencies:**
- Instead of referencing exact frequencies (e.g., 2.437 GHz), channels provide a simple identifier (e.g., **channel 6**).
- Example: On an access point, you might see 2.4 GHz channel 6 and 5 GHz channel 44.

**Wireless Bandwidth:**
- The width of the frequency range used for a channel.
- Common widths: 20 MHz, 40 MHz, 80 MHz, 160 MHz.
- Wider channels = higher potential throughput but more spectrum usage and interference risk.

---

#### Channel Availability by Band

| Band | Non-Overlapping Channels (20 MHz) | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **2.4 GHz** | 1, 6, 11 | Only three non-overlapping channels in most regulatory domains. |
| **5 GHz** | Many (varies by country) | Significant expansion over 2.4 GHz; supports wider channels. |
| **6 GHz** | Very many | Largest contiguous spectrum available for Wi-Fi; enables wide channels without interference. |

---

#### Band Steering

Band steering is a feature on access points that encourages dual-band clients to connect to a preferred frequency band.

| Without Band Steering | With Band Steering |
| :--- | :--- |
| Client chooses the band with the strongest signal (often 2.4 GHz). | AP steers clients to higher bands (e.g., 5 GHz or 6 GHz) for better throughput. |
| May result in suboptimal performance if 2.4 GHz is congested. | Optimizes spectrum usage by balancing clients across bands. |

**Benefit:** Higher-band frequencies (5 GHz, 6 GHz) generally offer more channels, wider bandwidth, and less interference, even if signal strength is slightly lower than 2.4 GHz.

---

#### Regulatory Compliance and Interoperability

Wireless spectrum is managed by government agencies in each country (e.g., **FCC** in the United States). International coordination ensures devices can operate globally while respecting local regulations.

| Feature | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **802.11h** | Standard that adds interoperability features for spectrum management, enabling better coexistence in crowded environments. |
| **DFS (Dynamic Frequency Selection)** | Allows access points to use frequencies shared with radar systems. APs must detect radar and automatically switch channels to avoid interference. |
| **TPC (Transmit Power Control)** | Allows the access point to instruct clients to adjust their transmit power, reducing interference with other devices and services. |

**How These Work Together:**
- When you power on an AP, it scans for existing networks and selects optimal channels automatically (no manual configuration).
- If interference is detected (e.g., radar on a DFS channel), the AP changes channels and notifies connected clients to follow.
- TPC ensures clients use only the necessary power, minimizing interference with neighboring networks and services.

---

#### Key Takeaways

| Takeaway | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Wi-Fi Generations Simplify Naming** | Wi-Fi 5 (ac), Wi-Fi 6/6E (ax), and Wi-Fi 7 (be) are the current standards. |
| **Three Primary Frequency Bands** | 2.4 GHz (crowded but good range), 5 GHz (more channels, higher throughput), 6 GHz (newest, widest channels). |
| **Channels Simplify Frequency Reference** | Instead of exact frequencies, channels like "channel 6" are used. Channel widths can be 20–160 MHz. |
| **Band Steering Optimizes Client Connections** | APs can steer clients to higher bands (5 GHz or 6 GHz) for better performance. |
| **Regulatory Compliance is Critical** | Agencies like the FCC manage spectrum; features like DFS and TPC ensure Wi-Fi coexists with other services (e.g., radar). |
| **Automatic Configuration is Standard** | Modern APs automatically select channels and adjust power to avoid interference without manual tuning. |
